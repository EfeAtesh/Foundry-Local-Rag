# Vehicle Fixing & Maintenance Guide
*Comprehensive Roadside and Garage Technical Manual for Diagnostics, Fluids, Brakes, and Ignition Systems.*

---

## Section 1: Safety Protocols & Required Tools

Before performing any vehicle inspection or repair, you must establish a safe working environment. Never compromise on safety procedures, especially when raising a vehicle.

### 1.1 Safety Rules
*   **Level Ground:** Always park the vehicle on a flat, solid concrete surface. Never jack up a car on dirt, gravel, or an incline.
*   **Parking Brake & Wheel Chocks:** Always engage the emergency parking brake. Place heavy-duty wheel chocks behind and in front of the tires on the opposite axle being lifted (e.g., chock the rear tires if lifting the front).
*   **Jack Stands:** Never work under a vehicle supported solely by a hydraulic floor jack. Floor jacks can fail catastrophically. Always lower the vehicle onto rated mechanical jack stands and test stability by shaking the car gently before crawling underneath.
*   **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Wear impact-resistant safety glasses at all times. Use nitrile gloves to protect skin from toxic fluids (oil, brake fluid) and heavy-duty work gloves for hot exhaust components.

### 1.2 Essential Tool Checklist
*   **Hand Tools:** Sockets (metric and standard), ratchets, torque wrench, screwdrivers, pliers, and wire brushes.
*   **Diagnostic Tools:** Digital Multimeter (DMM), OBD-II Code Scanner.
*   **Lifting Tools:** Hydraulic floor jack, rated jack stands, and wheel chocks.
*   **Fluid Tools:** Drain pan, oil filter wrench, funnel, and fluid extraction pump.

## Section 2: Engine Diagnostics (Starting Issues)

When an engine fails to start, follow this systematic diagnostic flowchart based on electrical and fuel systems.

```
                  [Turn Key to Ignition]
                            |
         +------------------+------------------+
         |                                     |
   (No Sound/Clicking)                  (Engine Cranks/Spins)
         |                                     |
[Check Battery & Starter]              [Check Fuel & Spark]
```

### 2.1 Case A: No Sound or Clicking
If the engine makes no sound or a rapid clicking sound when turning the key:
1.  **Test Battery Voltage:** Set your digital multimeter to DC voltage. Place the red lead on the positive battery terminal and the black lead on the negative terminal.
    *   *12.6V or above:* Battery is fully charged.
    *   *12.0V to 12.4V:* Battery is discharged. Needs charging or jump-starting.
    *   *Below 10.0V during cranking:* Internal battery cells are damaged; the battery must be replaced.
2.  **Inspect Battery Terminals:** Look for white, green, or blue powdery corrosion on the terminals. Clean terminals with a wire brush and a baking soda-water mixture. Ensure the clamps are tight and cannot be twisted by hand.
3.  **Starter Motor Test:** If the battery is healthy (12.6V) but only a single loud click is heard, the starter solenoid is engaging but the starter motor armature or starter relay has failed.

### 2.2 Case B: Engine Cranks but Won't Start (No-Start Condition)
If the engine spins healthily but does not catch fire, you are missing one of the three essentials: Fuel, Spark, or Compression.
1.  **Fuel Pump Check:** Turn the ignition key to the 'ON' position (do not crank). Listen closely near the rear seat or gas cap for a 2-second humming sound. If there is silence, the fuel pump or fuel pump relay has failed.
2.  **Fuel Pressure Test:** Hook a pressure gauge to the fuel rail Schrader valve. Verify if fuel pressure matches your vehicle's factory specifications (typically 35–50 PSI for port injection).
3.  **Ignition Spark Test:** Remove an ignition coil pack, insert a spark plug tester, ground it to the engine block, and crank the engine. Check for a bright blue spark. A yellow/weak spark indicates bad ignition coils or worn spark plugs.

## Section 3: Fluid Management & Preventative Maintenance

Maintaining proper fluid levels and clean lubricants prevents major engine wear and transmission failure.

### 3.1 Step-by-Step Engine Oil Change
Engine oil should be changed every 5,000 to 7,500 miles depending on synthetic specifications.
1.  **Warm up the engine:** Run the engine for 5 minutes so the oil warms up and flows easily. Shut down the engine.
2.  **Drain the oil:** Position a drain pan under the oil pan. Remove the oil drain plug using a socket wrench. Let the oil drain completely. Inspect the crush washer on the plug; replace it if flattened. Reinstall the plug and torque to factory specifications (usually 20–30 ft-lbs).
3.  **Replace the oil filter:** Use an oil filter wrench to remove the old filter. 
    *   *Critical Step:* Verify the old rubber gasket came off with the filter. If it remains stuck to the engine block (double-gasketing), the new filter will leak rapidly and destroy the engine.
    *   *Lubricate:* Lightly smear clean engine oil on the rubber seal of the new filter. Hand-tighten the filter until the gasket makes contact, then turn it an additional 3/4 turn. Do not over-tighten.
4.  **Refill:** Add the recommended oil weight (e.g., 5W-30) using a funnel. Run the engine for 2 minutes, check for leaks around the plug/filter, turn off the engine, wait 5 minutes, and verify the oil level on the dipstick is between the MIN and MAX lines.

### 3.2 Antifreeze/Coolant Management
*   **Coolant Mixture:** Antifreeze must always be mixed 50/50 with distilled water to prevent freezing, boiling, and corrosion. Never use tap water as minerals will scale the radiator.
*   **System Bleeding:** After replacing coolant, you must bleed trapped air out of the system. Run the engine with the radiator cap off using a spill-free funnel until the radiator fan turns on twice. Watch for bubbles exiting the funnel.

### 3.3 Brake Fluid Inspection
*   **DOT Rating:** Only use the specific brake fluid type indicated on the reservoir cap (typically DOT 3 or DOT 4). Never mix silicone-based DOT 5 with glycol-based DOT 3/4.
*   **Moisture Testing:** Brake fluid is hygroscopic (absorbs moisture from the air). Use a electronic brake fluid tester to check water content. If moisture content exceeds 3%, flush the brake lines immediately to prevent vapor lock and internal corrosion.

## Section 4: Brake System Maintenance

Brakes are the most critical safety system on any vehicle. Inspect pad thickness and rotor conditions at every oil change.

### 4.1 Brake Pad Replacement Checklist
*   **Thickness Limit:** Brake pads must be replaced if the friction material is less than 3mm (approx. 1/8 inch) thick.
*   **Caliper Slide Pins:** Clean slide pins with brake cleaner, check for binding or pitting, and lubricate them with high-temperature silicone grease. Frozen slide pins cause uneven pad wear and brake drag.
*   **Rotor Runout:** Inspect brake rotors for deep scoring, rust ridges, or warping. Warped rotors cause steering wheel vibration during braking. Always torque wheel lug nuts in a star pattern to prevent rotor warping.

### 4.2 Brake Bleeding Procedure
Always bleed brakes in order of distance from the master cylinder (furthest to closest):
1.  **Right Rear (Passenger Rear)**
2.  **Left Rear (Driver Rear)**
3.  **Right Front (Passenger Front)**
4.  **Left Front (Driver Front)**

*Procedure:* Have an assistant pump the brake pedal 3 times and hold it down. Open the bleeder valve on the caliper, let fluid and air bubbles escape into a transparent tube submerged in clean brake fluid, close the valve, and tell the assistant to release the pedal. Repeat until no air bubbles appear, monitoring the master cylinder reservoir to ensure it doesn't run dry.

## Section 5: Spark Plugs & Ignition Coils

Worn ignition components cause engine misfires, poor fuel economy, and catalytic converter damage.

### 5.1 Spark Plug Inspection
*   **Gap Measurement:** Use a spark plug gap tool to measure the distance between the center and ground electrodes. Verify against the vehicle emission control label (typically between 0.028 to 0.044 inches).
*   **Reading Spark Plugs:**
    *   *Light tan/gray deposits:* Healthy combustion.
    *   *Heavy black carbon soot:* Engine running too rich (excess fuel or insufficient air).
    *   *Wet engine oil:* Leaking valve stem seals or worn piston rings.
    *   *Blistered white ceramic:* Engine running too lean (too much air, overheating, incorrect heat range spark plug).

## Section 6: OBD-II Diagnostic Trouble Codes (DTC)

When the Check Engine Light (MIL) illuminates, connect a code scanner to the OBD-II port located under the driver's side dashboard.

### 6.1 Common Diagnostic Trouble Codes (DTCs)

| Code | Meaning | Common Causes | Diagnostic Steps |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **P0300** | Random/Multiple Cylinder Misfire | Bad spark plugs, failed ignition coil, vacuum leak, low fuel pressure. | Run a cylinder misfire test. Check ignition coils. |
| **P0171** | System Too Lean (Bank 1) | Vacuum leak, dirty Mass Airflow Sensor (MAF), clogged fuel filter. | Inspect vacuum hoses for cracks. Clean MAF sensor. |
| **P0172** | System Too Rich (Bank 1) | Leaking fuel injector, stuck open thermostat, failed O2 sensor. | Check fuel pressure. Monitor O2 sensor voltage. |
| **P0420** | Catalyst System Efficiency Below Threshold | Failed catalytic converter, exhaust leak, oxygen sensor failure. | Check exhaust manifold for cracks. Compare upstream and downstream O2 sensor wave patterns. |
