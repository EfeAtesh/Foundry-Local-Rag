# Guide to Finding Water and Setting Fire
*Based on wilderness survival science and primitive engineering guides.*

## Part 1: Procuring and Purifying Water
Finding water is your highest priority in a survival situation. Do not drink untreated water unless it is a life-or-death emergency.

### 1. Finding Water Sources
*   **Topography:** Look for green vegetation patches or follow birds and animal tracks, which often lead downhill towards water basins or streams.
*   **Solar Condensation (Belowground Still):** Dig a hole 3 feet wide and 2 feet deep in damp soil or a dry riverbed. Place a container in the center. Cover the hole with a plastic sheet, sealing the edges with soil. Place a small stone in the center of the sheet to create a cone shape pointing down to the container. Solar heat will vaporize soil moisture, which condenses on the plastic and drips into your container.

### 2. Purifying Water
*   **Boiling:** The absolute safest method. Bring water to a rolling boil for at least 1 full minute (3 minutes at high altitudes above 2000 meters) to kill all pathogens, bacteria, and parasites.
*   **Improvised Charcoal Filter:** Cut a plastic bottle in half. Pack the neck with cloth or grass. Layer crushed charcoal (from a campfire) on top of the grass, then add a layer of sand, and a layer of pebbles. Pour muddy water through the top. The water will filter through the layers and run clear out of the bottom. *Note: This filters particles but does not kill bacteria; you must still boil the output.*

## Part 2: Firecraft (Setting Fire)
Fire provides warmth, light, signaling, cooking, and water purification.

### 1. The Fire Triangle
To build a fire, you need three elements: Fuel, Heat (ignition source), and Oxygen.
*   **Tinder:** Extremely dry, fine materials that catch a spark (dried grass, birch bark, wood shavings, lint).
*   **Kindling:** Small twigs and split wood (pencil size) that catch fire from the tinder.
*   **Fuel Wood:** Larger branches and logs that keep the fire burning.

### 2. Primitive Ignition Methods
*   **Flint and Steel:** Strike a high-carbon steel striker against a sharp piece of flint at a 45-degree angle. Direct the sparks onto a dry char cloth or tinder bundle. Blow gently once a glowing ember forms.
*   **Bow Drill (Friction Fire):** Use a curved bow to rotate a wooden spindle rapidly against a flat hearth board. The friction creates wood powder that heats up and forms a glowing ember. Transfer this ember into a dry tinder nest and blow it into a flame.
